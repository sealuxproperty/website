Legal guide

Can you buy property in Thailand while living in Europe?

Yes. Your country of residence doesn't determine your eligibility — it's your nationality and the source of your funds that matter. Here's exactly how that works for European buyers.

Yes — living in Europe does not stop you from buying a condominium in Thailand, and it does not change the rules that apply to you. Ownership eligibility in Thailand is governed by nationality and (in Thailand's case) immigration and funds-source requirements — not by where you currently live. The practical parts of the process that residence does touch — how you move money, how you sign documents remotely, and what you owe your home tax authority afterward — are covered below.

The rule that actually governs this: nationality, not residence

Thailand's Condominium Act B.E. 2522 (1979), Section 19, sets out who can own a condo unit freehold. Multiple independent legal sources confirm there is no restriction tied to nationality, and none tied to country of residence — the qualifying tests are about your immigration status and where the purchase money comes from, not where you happen to live day to day.

In practice this means a Canadian, American, British, or European buyer is treated identically under Thai law, whether they live in their home country, in Thailand, or somewhere else entirely. What actually matters is (1) that you can legally enter Thailand, and (2) that the purchase funds are remitted into Thailand from overseas in foreign currency.

The 49% foreign quota

Foreign ownership across a condominium building cannot exceed 49% of total floor area, calculated at registration, not at reservation. Get written confirmation of current quota status from the developer or the building's juristic person before paying a deposit.

The funds requirement (this is the part residence actually affects — logistically)

To register freehold ownership you must prove the purchase price was remitted into Thailand from abroad in foreign currency. The receiving Thai bank issues a Foreign Exchange Transaction Form (FET, historically the Tor Tor 3), which is required at the Land Office alongside your passport and the sale and purchase agreement.

Living abroad doesn't create a legal obstacle here — if anything it's the normal case the rule was written for, since the money has to originate outside Thailand anyway. The transfer should be made from an account in your own name, and the payment reference should state the purpose as a condominium purchase, ideally citing the unit number.

Buying without flying to Thailand

You do not need to be physically present in Thailand for the purchase or the Land Office transfer. Overseas buyers commonly complete the deal remotely by appointing a lawyer or representative under a Land Department power of attorney, signed and notarised in their home country and then legalised by a Royal Thai embassy or consulate — Thailand is not a party to the Hague Apostille Convention, so a standard apostille alone is generally not accepted for this purpose.

The exact POA form and process should be confirmed with the lawyer handling your transfer, since different sources describe slightly different form numbers for condos versus land — don't take a form number from a blog post as final; have your lawyer confirm the current Land Department requirement directly.

The honest answer on tax and reporting

“Europe” isn't a single tax jurisdiction, so we can't give you one rule here the way we can for Canada, the US, or the UK. Each EU country — and non-EU European country — sets its own rules on whether, and how, resident taxpayers must declare foreign real estate, foreign rental income, and capital gains on its sale, and whether a double taxation agreement with Thailand or the Philippines applies to your situation.

We don't have a verified, country-specific source to cite here without knowing which country you're resident in, and we're not going to guess. If you're a tax resident of an EU country, confirm the reporting requirements with a local accountant or tax adviser before you buy, not after — some countries require annual disclosure of foreign assets, and penalties for late disclosure can be significant.

This is general information, not legal or tax advice

Property law in Thailand and tax rules in Europe both change, and your specific situation (marital status, how you fund the purchase, whether you plan to rent the unit out) can shift the answer. Before you commit, get independent advice from a property lawyer in Thailand and, separately, a tax adviser who understands European reporting obligations for foreign-held assets.
