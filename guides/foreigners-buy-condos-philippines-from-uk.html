Legal guide

Can you buy property in the Philippines while living in the United Kingdom?

Yes. Your country of residence doesn't determine your eligibility — it's your nationality and the source of your funds that matter. Here's exactly how that works for UK buyers.

Yes — living in the United Kingdom does not stop you from buying a condominium in the Philippines, and it does not change the rules that apply to you. Ownership eligibility in the Philippines is governed by nationality and (in Thailand's case) immigration and funds-source requirements — not by where you currently live. The practical parts of the process that residence does touch — how you move money, how you sign documents remotely, and what you owe your home tax authority afterward — are covered below.

The rule that actually governs this: it's constitutional, and residence is irrelevant

The Philippine Constitution reserves land ownership to Filipino citizens and to corporations that are at least 60% Filipino-owned. Foreign nationals cannot own land, full stop — not through a Canadian passport, a US passport, a UK passport, or an EU passport, and not by virtue of living in the Philippines, part-time or full-time.

Condominium units are the carve-out. Under the Condominium Act (Republic Act No. 4726), foreign nationals may own condo units outright, provided foreign ownership in that specific project does not exceed 40% of total units (or floor area). This applies equally regardless of where the buyer lives — the restriction is about citizenship, not residence, and no bilateral treaty overrides it for any of the countries you asked about.

The 40% quota

Each condominium project is capped individually. Popular developments in Metro Manila and Cebu can fill their foreign allocation quickly, so confirm current quota availability with the developer or a licensed broker before paying a reservation fee — title cannot be registered to a foreign buyer once a project's cap is exceeded.

Financing note

Philippine banks generally do not extend mortgage financing to non-resident foreign buyers, regardless of which country they live in. Purchases by foreign nationals living abroad are typically structured as cash purchases or developer in-house payment plans — factor this into your budgeting rather than assuming a local mortgage will be available.

Buying without flying to the Philippines

You do not need to be physically present. The standard route is a Special Power of Attorney (SPA) appointing a representative in the Philippines to sign on your behalf. The SPA must be notarised in your country of residence, then authenticated for use in the Philippines — via apostille if your country is a party to the Hague Apostille Convention (Canada, the US, the UK, and virtually all EU member states now are), or via consular authentication at a Philippine embassy or consulate if not.

Developers and agents commonly support the rest of the process — reservation, bank transfer, virtual site viewing — remotely as well.

What HMRC expects from you afterward

UK residents are taxed on worldwide income, so this isn't a case of “the property is abroad, so it's outside UK tax” — if you rent the unit out, that income goes on the foreign pages (SA106) of your Self Assessment return, converted to GBP. If you later sell, any capital gain is reportable to HMRC too, either through Self Assessment or the real-time Capital Gains Tax service.

Where the same income or gain is also taxed locally in Thailand or the Philippines, Foreign Tax Credit Relief is generally available to avoid being taxed twice on it — but the mechanics depend on your specific circumstances, so this is worth confirming with an accountant rather than assuming it applies automatically.

This is general information, not legal or tax advice

Property law in the Philippines and tax rules in the United Kingdom both change, and your specific situation (marital status, how you fund the purchase, whether you plan to rent the unit out) can shift the answer. Before you commit, get independent advice from a property lawyer in the Philippines and, separately, a tax adviser who understands UK reporting obligations for foreign-held assets.
